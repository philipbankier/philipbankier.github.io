02 · KEY FINDINGS FROM RESEARCH
◆ ACTIVATION STEERING IS UNDERRATED
Extract a 'voice vector' from 50-100 paired examples. No training, no weights changed. Zhang et al. (March 2025): 8% quality gain over RAG with a few-KB file. Works on any open model.
◆ ACE SOLVES THE FROZEN-TASTE PROBLEM
Stanford's ACE treats context as an evolving playbook with structured delta updates. +10.6% on agents. Prevents context collapse that kills naive self-improving prompts. Open-source.
◆ SLEEP-TIME COMPUTE ENABLES OFFLINE LEARNING
Letta's April 2025 paper: agents that reflect during downtime, consolidate memories, and precompute insights. 18% accuracy gain, 2.5× cost reduction. Like human sleep consolidation.
◆ PERSONA VECTORS ARE AUTOMATED
Anthropic (July 2025): give a natural-language trait description → get a steering vector automatically. Correlations as high as 0.97 between vector activation and trait expression. Code is open.
◆ GEPA > HAND-WRITTEN PROMPTS, ALWAYS
Genetic-Pareto prompt evolution (DSPy, July 2025) outperforms reinforcement learning on multiple benchmarks. Give it 30 examples + a metric → get a better prompt than you'd ever write.
◆ LETTA SHIPS PORTABILITY TODAY
Agent File (.af) format serializes memory + skills + persona into a portable bundle across models. Letta Code decouples agent identity from provider. Closest to what you actually want.

07 · THE THESIS

The techniques exist.
The product doesn't.

Activation steering, ACE playbooks, sleep-time reflection, KTO feedback, and portable agent formats each independently solve one piece of the problem. They live in three disconnected research communities — mechanistic interpretability, agent memory, and automatic prompt optimization — that haven't merged into a single consumer product.

The opportunity for someone willing to integrate these pieces is wide open. The gap in the upper-right quadrant of the landscape chart — high effectiveness, high accessibility — is where the next important product will be.

THREE COMMUNITIES, DISCONNECTED

Klarna handles 80% of all marketing copywriting with AI at 70% cost reduction, cutting its marketing team from 200 to 100 people while running more campaigns. Adore Me achieves a 95/5 AI-to-human split on stylist notes using Writer.com's brand voice profiles. A Forrester TEI study found that half of Jasper AI's outputs were on-brand on the first attempt, reducing rework by 50%. Claude Skills users report that "the first output is already 80% there."

These results are genuinely impressive for structured, formulaic content — social media captions, product descriptions, email templates, ad copy variations. The pattern holds across tools: Claude Skills, Custom GPTs, Jasper brand voice, Writer.com voice profiles, and bespoke enterprise systems all deliver meaningful productivity gains when the target voice can be reduced to concrete rules.

But this success is domain-constrained. A Jasper user's Trustpilot review cuts to the problem: "The Brand Voice does NOT carry through, it reverts back to AI speak." Fahim AI's hands-on testing found Jasper "great for social media captions and online ads" but disappointing for long-form pieces where "every article needed heavy editing." The consistent signal is that instruction-based voice transfer works for short, structured, pattern-heavy content and degrades as outputs grow longer, more creative, or more tonally complex.

Every major framework for understanding expert knowledge converges on the same conclusion: explicit rules capture perhaps 20–30% of what constitutes taste. The remaining 70–80% is tacit — inarticulate, embodied, acquired through immersion rather than instruction.

Polanyi's Paradox

Michael Polanyi's foundational insight — "we can know more than we can tell" — describes exactly the challenge of writing a skill file for creative voice. A skilled editor knows instantly when a sentence "feels wrong" but may struggle to articulate why.

The Dreyfus Inversion

Stuart Dreyfus's skill acquisition model makes the irony explicit: experts move beyond rule-following to intuitive pattern recognition, so asking them to write rules for an LLM is asking them to regress to the novice-level articulation they transcended years ago.

Klein's Recognition-Primed Decisions

Gary Klein's research with firefighters and military commanders found that of 134 expert decision points studied, 117 used rapid pattern recognition rather than analytical comparison — the experts insisted they didn't "make decisions," they just knew.

Bourdieu's Habitus

The deepest theoretical challenge: taste isn't a set of preferences you can list but an embodied disposition formed through years of cultural immersion. You cannot inject habitus through a system prompt any more than you can become a sommelier by reading a manual.

The Critical Nuance

Yet the case isn't entirely damning. LLMs already possess vast tacit knowledge from pre-training. The practical insight from Slite's engineering team: you can't put tacit knowledge in a prompt, but you can activate tacit knowledge the model already has by referencing authorities and conventions it has absorbed. The gap between "injecting new knowledge" and "activating existing knowledge" is the gap between what rules can and cannot do.

Works Well

Marketing copywriting, UX writing, technical/academic writing, customer support. These domains share: relatively short outputs, pattern-heavy formats, well-established conventions, and taste that is more institutional than personal. Gorgias data shows people identify AI-generated service responses as AI only 46.9% of the time — below chance.

Partially Works

Long-form journalism (Bloomberg's Cyborg handles earnings reports but not analysis), brand design direction, teaching materials, nonfiction books. Author Kenny Kane finds Claude "exceptional at mirroring your style once it's seen enough examples" but notes it "occasionally over-edits, sanding off voice and rhythm in pursuit of clarity."

Largely Fails

Literary fiction (GPT models produce prose that is "too flowery, excessive, or over the top"), humor/comedy (depends on timing, subversion, audience-specific context), poetry (rhythm, sound, the unsaid), editorial judgment (knowing what story to tell, what angle to take). Music composition, video editing, and photography curation involve embodied, multimodal aesthetic judgments that text-based instructions capture only at the most superficial level.

The pattern: the more a domain's quality depends on personal voice, cultural context, and the strategic deployment of surprise, the worse instruction-based taste transfer performs.

I. Voice Dissolves Across Long Outputs

Every transformer tested degrades as context grows. Softmax attention normalizes weights across all tokens, so each style instruction receives proportionally less attention. Stanford's "lost-in-the-middle" research found accuracy drops 30%+ when relevant information sits in the middle of context. The ICML 2024 paper on persona drift found persona self-consistency metrics degrade by more than 30% after 8–12 dialogue turns.

II. The Articulation Paradox

Shrivu Shankar: "Out of the box, many of us can't fully articulate why we prefer one design over another." Nielsen Norman Group confirmed users "lack the vocabulary or knowledge necessary to articulate some components precisely." The ghostwriter behind Tastemaker.bot: "My clients always knew when something was off. 'This doesn't sound like me.' But they couldn't say why." This creates a selection bias: only rule-followable taste gets captured.

III. Shared Instructions Homogenize Output at Population Scale

Doshi and Hauser (Science Advances 2024): writers given AI ideas produced individually more creative but collectively more similar stories. A "creative scar" follow-up found homogeneity keeps climbing months after AI withdrawal. Cornell's CHI 2025 study found AI autocomplete caused Indian participants' writing to become more Westernized. Words like "delve" and "robust" spiked 50%+ in published writing.

IV. Negative Constraints Are Unreliable

Creative taste is often defined by restraint. But ICML 2025 research found positive rules achieved highest adherence while negative prohibitions "break down in the face of conflicting goals." The 16x.engineer "Pink Elephant Problem" analysis confirmed this at scale. Anthropic's own documentation advises "tell Claude what to do instead of what not to do."

V. Static Instructions Freeze Your Taste

Human taste evolves; instruction files don't. After a rebrand, "the 47 saved prompts still contain old-voice instructions. For three months, content oscillates." Underlying models change too. CLAUDE.md files grow to 800+ lines and the model starts ignoring them. Shankar: "alpha decay" — your taste advantage resets every few months.

Chakrabarty and Dhillon (2026): 28 MFA-program writers competed against LLMs emulating 50 acclaimed authors. With in-context prompting, expert judges preferred human writing 82.7% of the time. After fine-tuning on the authors' complete works, judges preferred the AI output 62% of the time. Fine-tuning flipped the result.

Sarah Constantin: prompting is "lousy for making a bot with a writing style substantially different from generic ChatGPT" but fine-tuning on 124 Frank Herbert quotes produced output with "a clearly Frank-Herbert-inspired style." Key insight: style includes conversational attitude — the fine-tuned bot would spontaneously argue with and belittle the user, a behavior nearly impossible to elicit from prompting.

LLMs are architecturally exemplar-biased — they learn through pattern matching from examples, not rule application. PNAS found GPT-J learns through "token-level analogical generalization." Few-shot examples produce 23.5× higher style-matching scores than zero-shot prompting.

Practical hierarchy: hybrid (fine-tuning + rules + examples) > fine-tuning alone > sophisticated few-shot > basic few-shot > RAG > explicit rules > iterative feedback alone.

Activation Steering / Contrastive Activation Addition

Take paired examples, run through a model, extract the difference between hidden state activations, inject during inference. No training. Zhang et al. (March 2025): beats RAG by ~8% with a few KB per user. SteerX (October 2025) extends with style descriptions. Requires open models (Llama, Qwen, Gemma, DeepSeek) via Together AI or similar.

Sparse Autoencoder Feature Steering

Goodfire/DeepMind SAEs decompose activations into ~16,000-65,000 monosemantic features. Find features that fire for your writing style, amplify them. AxBench (January 2025): naive SAE steering loses to prompting, but newer methods (SAE-RSV, CorrSteer, FGAA) close the gap. Human-readable knobs: features labeled "uses concrete sensory detail," etc. Goodfire's Ember API is commercial.

Anthropic's Persona Vectors

July 2025. Automated: trait description → steering vector. Correlations up to 0.97. "Preventative steering" during training works better than post-hoc. Code: github.com/safety-research/persona_vectors. Could define your expert's best work as a trait and extract the vector.

ACE — Agentic Context Engineering

Stanford/SambaNova/Berkeley, October 2025. Evolving playbook: Generator → Reflector → Curator with structured delta items. Defends against brevity bias and context collapse. +10.6% on agents. Works without labels. Code: github.com/ace-agent/ace. Fork this for personal taste transfer.

Sleep-Time Compute

Letta + Berkeley, April 2025. Agents reflect during downtime. 18% accuracy gain, 2.5× cost reduction. "Sleeper agent" processes past conversations, "serve agent" uses learned context at query time. Your taste agent could reflect on your past work while you sleep.

KTO — Kahneman-Tversky Optimization

Binary "this is me / this isn't me" feedback instead of paired comparisons. Grounded in prospect theory. SimPO outperforms DPO by 6.4 points. The plumbing exists (Together AI), but no consumer product wraps it yet.

Many-Shot ICL

Google DeepMind, NeurIPS 2024 Spotlight. Hundreds/thousands of examples in-context. Key finding: in many-shot, example selection matters less — random nearly matches retrieval-based. Dumping your entire archive into context may work better than curating 5 examples.

DSPy / GEPA

Stanford's DSPy treats prompts as programs to optimize. GEPA (July 2025): genetic-Pareto reflective evolution outperforms RL. Define task + metric + 30-300 examples → algorithmically evolved prompt. Highest-leverage thing most people could try this week.

Agent Memory Architectures

Explosive research: episodic → semantic consolidation, procedural memory distillation. Key systems: Letta/MemGPT (self-editing memory), Mem0 (80% token reduction, 26% higher quality), MemMachine (93% accuracy). "Remember Me, Refine Me" formalizes skill life cycles. TiMem introduces temporal-hierarchical trees for personalization without fine-tuning.

Letta / Agent File Format

Agent File (.af) serializes stateful agents — memory + skills + persona — portably across Claude, GPT, Gemini. Letta Code (late 2025): /remember command for skill learning. Git-backed memory = version control over taste evolution. The only group productionizing statefulness as a first-class concern.

Naive LoRA on small corpus (<500 examples)

LaMP benchmark: PEFT alone = 1.07% improvement; RAG = 14.92%; combined = 15.98%. Fine-tuning a small LoRA is worse than retrieving from the same corpus. Exception: 1000+ high-quality examples.

Multi-property vector addition

Adding steering vectors together fails due to feature entanglement (Weij 2024). Solution: simultaneous layer-wise injection at distinct layers.

Self-improving prompts without structured updates

Context collapse: iterative rewriting erodes details. Builder.io CLAUDE.md guide and MLOps "prompt bloat" article document the same failure: prompts grow to 800+ lines, model ignores them. ACE's structured delta-item approach was specifically designed to prevent this.

Steering from too few examples

Under 50-100 paired examples = noisy activations capturing spurious correlations. SAE-RSV (September 2025) denoises using sparse autoencoders to separate task-relevant from irrelevant features.

Style fine-tuning without preserving instruction-following

StyleTunedLM (September 2024): fine-tuning for style quietly kills instruction compliance. Solution: separate instruction LoRA (LIMA dataset) merged with style LoRA.

LLM-based imitation of ordinary authors

"Catch Me If You Can? Not Yet" (September 2025): 40,000+ generations, 400+ authors. Human-passing rate below 20% in most settings. GPT-4o near-zero by GPTZero. Faithfully imitating implicit individual style from few examples "remains a challenging task."

Best single-action recommendation

Take your best 200-500 pieces and build a personal RAG index (with annotations on why each worked) combined with a managed fine-tune on the same corpus every six months. RAG handles recency and examples; fine-tune bakes in deep cadence and attitude. Total cost: $50-200 per tuning run. Portability: the corpus is yours forever. Continual learning: add new pieces immediately, re-tune quarterly.

If you want to push the frontier

Fork ACE: adapt the Reflector to read draft-acceptance/rejection as feedback. Replace "did the code execute" with "did the user keep this draft." Curator builds structured taste playbook. Combine with Letta's .af format for portability. Add sleep-time agent for periodic reflection. This is ~a weekend to prototype and a genuine research contribution.

The honest gap that remains

Even the best combination captures the articulable 60-70% of an expert's taste. The remaining gap is editorial judgment — knowing which piece to write, which angle to take, which detail to foreground. No currently-shippable tool closes it. The 80/20 pattern isn't a sign the last 20% needs better prompting. It's a sign it may be structurally inaccessible to instruction-based approaches, residing in exactly the tacit knowledge that Polanyi told us we can know but cannot tell.
